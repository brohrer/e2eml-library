There’s nothing quite as intimate is choosing your next data science project. If it’s not being handed to you by a teacher or a manager or a program manager, then the world is just too wide. How do you choose what to work on next? What data to explore? What problems to tackle? It can be overwhelming. Like all questions of importance, I don’t have the answer. But if you are feeling well and truly lost, here are some tricks that may get you unstuck. Find a question to answer Applied machine learning and data center science of all varieties is an exercise in answering a question using data. No matter how much data you have, You can’t exercise your data science skills without a question to answer. Choosing a project then, is actually the act of finding a question to answer. Focus on this Goals drive questions Asking a question is the act of getting closer to a goal. If my goal it’s a warm loaf of bread, then my question becomes how do I get to the bakery? Goals Drive questions. If you don’t have a burning question already, the fastest way to find one is to choose a goal. What do you want? This is the most personal very personal. Do you want to make a room full of money? Stop the spread of COVID-19? Build a company with 1000 employees? Understand the origin of the universe? Find A city that you want to move to? What you want defines who you are. Nobody can tell you what that is. If you are able to see deep enough into your own self to figure out what that is, then you will have a never Ending fountain of questions. Not all of these will be amenable to analysis, or will have appropriate data available, but you will have direction, and you will always be able to find an analysis or modeling question that gets you a little closer to that goal. It doesn’t matter what it is. The easiest way to do this is to steal someone else’s. Re-create their Tatie Tuesday data analysis. Implement a paper from scratch. Find a machine learning textbook and answer the questions posed at the end of each chapter. Stealing questions from others is 100% ethical. Not only that it’s flattering to the original question to ask her, and as a bonus they provide you with someone else’s answers to compare against. It lets you stretch your legs and get comfortable with new tools. It is a great way to learn to walk Before you start to run. Keep in mind however that all of this is practice. The most important thing you will do as a data scientist war machine learning practitioner is to choose the right questions to ask. Good decisions come from experience, and experience comes from bad decisions. good questions come from experience and experience comes from bad questions. There is no field guide, no manual, no cheat sheet for learning to ask good questions. It comes through trial and error. The best way to climb this learning curve is to take the first step. Want to know a thing. Pose the question. Does data exist that might provide an answer? What method might best pull the answer from the data? When someone asks, what should I work on?
